What Are Progressive Web Applications?

A look at where PWAs came from, what they are, and why they matter

Definition and Background

A Progressive Web Application is a type of web application that uses modern web capabilities to deliver a user experience similar to a native app. The term was coined by Google engineers Alex Russell and Frances Berriman in 2015, when they described a set of principles that any web app could adopt to become more capable and reliable.

The word "progressive" is intentional. It means the app works for every user regardless of browser or device, but takes advantage of additional features when they are available. On a modern browser with a good connection the experience is fast and full-featured. On an older browser or slow connection, it still works — just with fewer capabilities. This is called progressive enhancement.

Before PWAs, web developers had two options: build a website that works everywhere but feels limited on mobile, or build a native app that works great but requires app stores, separate codebases for iOS and Android, and asking users to download something before they can even try it. PWAs offer a third path.

Three Core Characteristics

Reliable

A PWA loads instantly even under poor network conditions or when there is no connection at all. This is made possible by service workers, which intercept network requests and serve cached content when the network is unavailable.

Fast

PWAs respond quickly to user interactions with smooth animations and fast load times. Google research found that 53% of mobile users abandon a site that takes more than three seconds to load. PWAs address this with pre-caching and app shell architecture.

Engaging

PWAs can be installed to a device's home screen without an app store. Once installed, they can send push notifications, run in fullscreen, and behave just like a native app from the user's perspective.

How PWAs Compare to Native Apps

For most web applications — news sites, e-commerce, social media, productivity tools — a PWA can match or exceed the native app experience while costing significantly less to build and maintain. One codebase works across all platforms instead of maintaining separate iOS and Android apps.

Pinterest rebuilt its mobile web experience as a PWA and saw a 60% increase in core engagements and a 44% increase in user-generated ad revenue compared to their previous mobile web experience (Google Developers, 2017).

Companies That Use PWAs

  • Twitter Lite — reduced data usage by 70%, increased tweets sent by 75%
  • Pinterest — 60% increase in core engagements after PWA rebuild
  • Starbucks — PWA is 99.84% smaller than their iOS app
  • Uber — PWA loads in under 3 seconds on 2G networks

Browser Support

PWA support has expanded significantly since 2015. Chrome, Edge, Firefox, and Safari all support core PWA features to varying degrees.
